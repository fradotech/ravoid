# Your Logs Cost More Than the App That Wrote Them

_By Framesta Fernando · Engineering Manager & Technical Architect · 10 min read · Published August 9, 2026_

> **TL;DR:** Logging cost at scale routinely exceeds the infrastructure it observes. Logs bill twice, once to ingest and again to index, and most of the volume is noise no one ever queries. Sampling, filtering, and routing low-value logs to cheap storage before the premium store can cut the bill 90% or more without losing the signal you actually use.

Observability is supposed to make operations cheaper by helping you find and fix problems faster. At scale it often inverts: the tool meant to reduce operational cost becomes one of the biggest line items on the infrastructure budget, larger than the systems it monitors ([DevOps.com on observability cost](https://devops.com/why-your-observability-stack-is-costing-you-more-than-your-cloud-bill/)). Teams discover that they are paying more to watch the application than to run it, and the reason is structural, not a mistake anyone made on purpose.

The structure is this: logs are billed twice and generated by default. Datadog, as the representative example, charges roughly $0.10 per GB to ingest plus a per-event indexing fee that makes logs searchable, which together push effective cost well above the headline ingest rate ([SigNoz on Datadog logs pricing](https://signoz.io/blog/datadog-logs-pricing)). And nobody decided to generate most of the volume: one request produces logs from each layer, metrics from each service, and dozens of spans in a distributed trace, multiplied by millions of requests a day, before anyone has decided whether all that data is useful ([Sawmills on the growing Datadog bill](https://www.sawmills.ai/blog/why-datadog-bill-keeps-growing)). Logging cost at scale is the bill for data you produced automatically and will mostly never read.

## The double charge nobody budgets for

The first surprise is that the per-GB ingest rate is not the real price. Logs are billed twice: ingestion at around $0.10 per GB, and indexing at roughly $1.27 to $2.50 per million events depending on retention, and even logs excluded from indexing still incur the ingestion cost ([SigNoz on reducing Datadog costs](https://signoz.io/guides/how-to-reduce-datadog-costs/)). Indexing is what makes a log searchable, dashboarded, and alertable, which is the whole point of sending it, so most teams index most of what they ingest and pay both charges on nearly everything.

Account for both and the blended rate lands around $1.80 per GB, so at 500 GB a day with 30-day retention the log bill reaches roughly $1,000,000 a year, while the same data on S3-backed storage costs about $12,600, a 98% difference ([Parseable on Datadog log cost](https://www.parseable.com/blog/datadog-log-management-cost)). The retention dimension is where budgets actually break: the cost per GB at ingest is the headline, but the cost to retain and search data over 30, 90, or 365 days is the real expense ([Big Data Boutique on log tools](https://bigdataboutique.com/blog/best-log-management-tools)). You are not paying to write a log. You are paying to keep it instantly searchable for a month, whether or not anyone ever searches it.

## The anchor: most of the volume has no reader

Here is the framing that reorders the problem. Log volume scales with how much your system emits, not with how much anyone reads, and those two numbers are wildly different. The vast majority of logs are debug lines, health checks, repeated stack traces, and routine success records that are never queried, never alerted on, and never looked at again. You pay premium ingest-plus-index rates to store data whose reader count is zero.

The proof is in what happens when teams actually measure it. Goldsky, a data platform, was ingesting 12 terabytes of logs a month into Datadog, and after analyzing what they actually needed for alerting and troubleshooting, they cut that to 795 gigabytes, with the other 11-plus terabytes being noise they were paying premium rates to store ([Grepr on observability pipeline costs](https://www.grepr.ai/blog/observability-pipeline-costs)). That is roughly 93% noise. Not 93% low-priority, 93% never useful for the purpose the logs existed to serve. This is the same distributed-default waste I keep returning to in [why SaaS teams overpay for infrastructure](https://ravoid.com/blog/why-saas-overpay-infrastructure): the system emits everything by default, and the default is what you pay for. I made the same case from the monitoring-architecture angle in [your Datadog bill is an architecture problem](https://ravoid.com/blog/datadog-cost-observability-trap).

| Log type | Share of volume | Query frequency | Worth premium indexing? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Errors / exceptions | Small | High | Yes |
| Warnings | Small | Medium | Often |
| Info / success | Large | Near zero | No |
| Debug / health checks | Largest | Effectively never | No |

## Sample and route before the meter

The fix is to stop sending everything to the premium store and decide value before the meter runs, not after. A log pipeline that can filter, transform, sample, and route logs before they hit the store ([Big Data Boutique](https://bigdataboutique.com/blog/best-log-management-tools)) is the difference between a $1M bill and a $100k one. Index the small high-value slice, route the rest to cheap object storage where it is still available for forensic search but costs almost nothing.

```yaml
# Decide value before the expensive store: index errors, archive the rest
pipeline:
  - match: { level: [error, fatal] }
    route: { sink: datadog_index }          # premium, searchable, alertable
  - match: { path: /healthz }
    route: { sink: drop }                     # health checks: never store
  - match: { level: debug }
    sample: 0.01                              # keep 1% of debug for context
    route: { sink: s3_archive }               # cheap, queryable on demand
  - default:
    route: { sink: s3_archive }               # info logs: archive, not index
```

Work the savings. Take an illustrative 500 GB/day at a blended $1.80/GB, about $27,000 a month. Apply Goldsky-style analysis where 93% is archivable noise: index the useful 35 GB/day at $1.80 and route 465 GB/day to S3-backed storage at roughly $0.03/GB blended.

```
Before: 500 GB/day x 30 x $1.80 = ~$27,000/month
After:
  Indexed:  35 GB/day x 30 x $1.80  = $1,890
  Archived: 465 GB/day x 30 x $0.03 = ~$420
  Total                              = ~$2,310/month
Saving: ~$24,700/month (~91%)
```

You did not lose any data, you still have the debug logs in S3 if an incident needs them, you just stopped paying premium real-time-search rates for data nobody searches in real time. This is the total-cost reasoning from [open source versus SaaS total cost of ownership](https://ravoid.com/blog/open-source-vs-saas-total-cost-ownership): match the tier of storage to the tier of access the data actually needs.

## Why elastic billing makes it worse

There is a second multiplier that catches cloud-native teams specifically. Observability platforms sell consumption (hosts monitored, data ingested, spans traced), and in cloud-native environments all of these units are elastic, so a Kubernetes cluster that auto-scales from 20 to 200 nodes under load is a 10x cost spike on a host-based model ([Atonement Licensing on observability licensing](https://atonementlicensing.com/blog/observability-licensing/)). The exact event that should make observability most valuable, a load spike or incident, is the event that most inflates its bill, often via a high-watermark model that charges for the peak.

That coupling, where cost scales with the incidents you most need to observe, is why "we'll watch the budget" never works for observability. The volume is driven by your system's behavior under stress, not by a budget decision, which is the same elasticity trap I described for vendor lock-in in [multi-cloud versus single-vendor hidden cost](https://ravoid.com/blog/multi-cloud-vs-single-vendor-hidden-cost). The only durable control is the pipeline: decide what to keep before the elastic meter counts it.

## A post-mortem on a million-dollar log bill

A composite from the documented pattern, with figures labeled illustrative: a company's observability bill grew to rival its entire compute spend, and leadership assumed the only options were paying it or losing visibility. An audit of what was actually queried told a different story. Roughly 90% of indexed log volume came from info-level and health-check logs that no dashboard referenced and no alert used, indexed at premium rates purely because logging defaults sent everything to the same place. The metric that broke was indexed-GB-per-queried-GB, which nobody tracked and which was wildly lopsided. Adding a log pipeline that dropped health checks, sampled debug logs, indexed only errors and warnings, and archived the rest to object storage cut the bill by over 85% within a month, with no loss of the signal the on-call team actually used during incidents.

## Framework: tier logs by how they are accessed

| Decision | What you gain | What you pay | When it breaks |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Index everything | Zero pipeline work | Premium rate on noise | Always, at scale |
| Index errors, archive rest | 80-90% savings | Pipeline to build/run | Need everything searchable live |
| Drop true noise (health checks) | Free savings | Care to confirm safe | Compliance log requirements |
| Sample high-volume debug | Keep context cheaply | Lose some detail | Need every debug line |

The order: drop logs nobody will ever need (health checks, liveness probes), sample high-volume low-value logs to keep statistical context cheaply, index only the errors and warnings that drive alerts and dashboards, and archive everything else to cheap storage where it is recoverable for forensics. The principle is that retention tier should match access pattern, and real-time premium search is for the small slice you query in real time.

## Decision guidance

The mistake is treating log volume as a fixed output of the system rather than a set of routing decisions you control before the bill is metered.

**The rule: If a log is not used by an alert, a dashboard, or routine debugging, then do not index it at premium rates, route it to cheap archival storage instead.**

The honest exception is regulated or security data with a genuine requirement to retain searchable logs for a defined period, where compliance dictates indexing and retention you cannot reduce. Even there, scope the requirement precisely, it usually covers specific event types, not your entire log firehose, so the compliance-mandated slice can be indexed while the rest is still archived. For everything else, indexing data with no reader is paying premium rates for storage you could get at a fraction of the cost.

## The watcher that outgrew the watched

Observability earns its place when it helps you find the one log line that explains an outage. It loses the plot when you are paying premium, indexed, real-time-searchable rates for the millions of log lines that explain nothing and that no one will ever read. The bill grows not because you are monitoring too carefully but because the defaults send everything to the most expensive tier, and the most expensive tier is sized for data you query, not data you emit.

The cheapest log is the one you archived to object storage instead of indexing, still there if you need it, costing almost nothing if you do not. Decide value before the meter runs, and the observability stack stops costing more than the application it was supposed to keep healthy.

## FAQ

### Q: Why is logging so expensive at scale?

Because logs are billed twice and generated by default. You pay to ingest each gigabyte and again to index it for search, alerting, and dashboards, pushing the effective rate well above the headline ingest price. Meanwhile every request emits logs, metrics, and traces from every layer automatically, so volume explodes before anyone decides the data is useful. At scale this can exceed the cost of the infrastructure being monitored.

### Q: What does Datadog log management actually cost?

Roughly $0.10 per GB to ingest plus about $1.27 to $2.50 per million events to index depending on retention, blending to around $1.80 per GB. At 500 GB a day with 30-day retention that reaches roughly $1,000,000 a year. The same data on S3-backed storage costs about $12,600, because object storage is not paying for premium real-time indexing and search you mostly do not use.

### Q: How much of my logs are actually useful?

Often a small fraction. One documented case cut log ingestion from 12 terabytes a month to 795 gigabytes, roughly 93% reduction, after analyzing what was truly needed for alerting and troubleshooting. The rest was debug lines, health checks, and routine success logs with no reader. Most teams find the majority of indexed volume is never queried, alerted on, or used in a dashboard.

### Q: How do I reduce logging costs without losing visibility?

Decide value before the expensive store. Drop true noise like health-check logs, sample high-volume debug logs to keep statistical context cheaply, index only errors and warnings that drive alerts and dashboards, and route everything else to cheap object storage where it remains available for forensic search. This commonly cuts the bill 85 to 90% while preserving the signal on-call engineers actually use during incidents.

### Q: What is the difference between log ingestion and indexing cost?

Ingestion is the fee for sending log data to the platform, around $0.10 per GB. Indexing is a separate per-event fee that makes those logs searchable, dashboardable, and alertable. Indexing is usually the larger cost. Importantly, logs excluded from indexing still incur ingestion charges, so filtering at the source, before ingestion, saves more than excluding logs from indexing alone.

### Q: Why does my observability bill spike during incidents?

Because observability platforms bill on elastic consumption units, hosts, ingested data, and spans, that all scale with system activity. A cluster that auto-scales from 20 to 200 nodes under load is a 10x cost spike on a host-based model, and incidents generate far more logs and traces. Many platforms use high-watermark billing that charges for the peak, so the events you most need to observe inflate the bill most.

### Q: Should I move logs to cheaper storage like S3?

For the large majority of logs that are not queried in real time, yes. Routing info-level, debug, and archival logs to S3-backed storage costs a fraction of premium indexing while keeping the data recoverable for forensics. Reserve premium real-time indexing for the small slice of errors and warnings that feed alerts and dashboards. The principle is matching storage tier to actual access pattern.
